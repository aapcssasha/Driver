What Happens if the U.S. Defaults: A Walk into Economic Abyss

Published: May 25, 2023 at 4:30 p.m. ET

It is one of those realities we like to pretend will never occur. A reality so grim, so perilous, that even the thought of it is enough to unsettle the hardest of hearts. Yet, it hangs in the balance, a veritable Sword of Damocles â€“ the possibility of the United States of America, the beacon of global economics, defaulting on its debts. What if the unthinkable happens? What if the world's largest economy defaults? Let's peer into this economic abyss.

The Day of Default: Day 0

The day the U.S. misses a payment on its debt marks the beginning of an unprecedented financial crisis. News broadcasts relay the information worldwide, shaking the confidence of investors everywhere. Stock markets around the globe react with swift and punishing sell-offs. The Dow Jones, S&P 500, and NASDAQ all plunge drastically, leading to one of the worst trading days in history.

Global Domino Effect: Week 1 - 2

The fallout is not contained within the borders of the U.S. The global economy, so deeply intertwined with the American economy, feels the shockwaves. As one of the safest investment instruments, U.S. Treasury bonds form the bedrock of global finance. Central banks worldwide, holding these bonds as part of their foreign exchange reserves, find their assets depreciated overnight. A wave of sell-offs begins, causing a significant drop in the U.S. dollar's value.

In the short run, commodities prices spike, given their inverse relationship with the dollar. The cost of oil, gold, and other commodities surge, leading to increased inflation worldwide, particularly in countries heavily dependent on imports.

Credit Crunch: Month 1

With the U.S. default, the global financial system experiences a credit crunch akin to the 2008 financial crisis, but potentially more severe. The lack of trust in the U.S. economy means that credit dries up. Banks become reluctant to lend, not only to each other but also to businesses and consumers. This, in turn, causes a significant slowdown in global economic activity, leading to recessions in numerous countries.

Rising Unemployment: Month 2 - 6

As businesses struggle to obtain credit, they start to cut costs. One of the most common ways to do this is by reducing the workforce. Unemployment begins to rise, first in the U.S. and then around the world. Consumer spending drops, leading to a vicious cycle of layoffs and reduced spending that further dampens the global economy.

Sovereign Debt Crisis: Month 6 - 12

After half a year of tumultuous economic activity, several countries that were already struggling with their debt burdens prior to the U.S. default face a severe sovereign debt crisis. Without the safety net of U.S. Treasury bonds and the faltering global economy, these countries find it impossible to service their debts.

This could potentially lead to a wave of defaults, causing the global economy to slide from a recession into a depression. The International Monetary Fund and the World Bank, already stretched thin, struggle to provide adequate support.

Economic Depression: Year 1 and Beyond

A year into the U.S. default, the world is likely in the throes of a significant economic depression. Unemployment remains high, many businesses have shuttered, and the global economy is stuck in a low-growth trap

Only the boldest of economic strategies, significant cooperation between nations, and fundamental reforms to the global financial system may set the stage for recovery. Yet, the path back to pre-default economic levels would be long and arduous, forever reminding the world of the consequences of a U.S. default.

Conclusion

While this scenario paints a daunting, almost apocalyptic picture, it is important to understand that it is hypothetical. Nonetheless, it is based on real potential consequences that could result from a U.S. default.

The world witnessed glimpses of this economic horror during the Great Recession of 2008. A U.S. default could lead to a financial crisis of greater magnitude. For this reason, the U.S. defaulting on its debt is considered an unthinkable event by most economists.

The U.S. economy is a crucial cornerstone of the global financial system. As such, its stability is paramount for the well-being of the global economy. Therefore, it is crucial for policymakers to take necessary measures to prevent such an event from ever occurring, ensuring that this narrative remains purely within the realm of speculation.

As we observe the economic landscape, the focus should be on creating robust financial systems that can withstand shocks, foster resilience, and ensure sustainable growth. Let us hope that the doomsday scenario outlined above remains a figment of our collective imagination, a tale spun around hypothetical scenarios, never to materialize in our reality. After all, the path to economic prosperity lies not in courting disaster, but in taking prudent steps today to prevent the calamities of tomorrow.
