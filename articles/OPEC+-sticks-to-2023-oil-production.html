OPEC+ sticks to 2023 oil production

Published: Jun 04, 2023 at 7:30 p.m. ET

Hello, dear readers! Welcome to another edition of Oil Watch, where we bring you the latest news and analysis on the world of oil and energy. Today, we have a special report on the OPEC+ meeting that took place in Vienna, where the group decided to stick to its 2023 oil production targets and Saudi Arabia announced further voluntary cuts. What does this mean for the oil market and the global economy? Let's find out!

First of all, let's recap what OPEC+ is and why it matters. OPEC+ is a group of 23 oil-producing countries that includes the 13 members of OPEC (Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries) and 10 other allies, such as Russia, Mexico and Kazakhstan. Together, they account for about 40% of global oil production and have a significant influence on oil prices and supply.

Since 2016, OPEC+ has been coordinating its output levels to balance the oil market and support prices, which have been affected by various factors such as oversupply, weak demand, geopolitical tensions and environmental concerns. The group has agreed on several production cuts and increases over the years, depending on the market conditions and the outlook for demand.

The latest agreement, reached in April 2021, was to gradually increase production by 2 million barrels per day (bpd) from May to July 2021, and then maintain a steady output level until April 2023. The agreement also included adjustments to the baseline levels that determine each country's output quota, giving some countries more room to produce more oil.

However, since then, the oil market has faced some challenges. The emergence of new variants of the coronavirus has raised fears of a slowdown in global economic recovery and oil demand. The uncertainty over the Iran nuclear deal and the possibility of more Iranian oil returning to the market has also weighed on prices. Moreover, some OPEC+ members have been struggling to comply with their quotas, either producing more or less than agreed.

All these factors have put pressure on oil prices, which have fallen from a two-year high of $77 per barrel in early July to around $70 per barrel at the time of writing. This has prompted some analysts and traders to speculate that OPEC+ might revise its production plans and either extend or deepen its cuts to support prices.

However, in a surprising move, OPEC+ decided to stick to its original plan and keep its output level unchanged until April 2023. The group said that it was confident in the prospects for oil demand growth and that it would continue to monitor the market closely. The group also praised Saudi Arabia for its "generous" decision to voluntarily cut an additional 400,000 bpd from August to December 2021, bringing its total voluntary cut to 1 million bpd.

What does this mean for the oil market and the global economy? Well, it depends on who you ask. Some experts say that OPEC+'s decision is a sign of optimism and stability, as it shows that the group is confident in the demand recovery and is not worried about oversupply or price volatility. They argue that this will benefit both producers and consumers, as it will ensure a steady supply of oil at reasonable prices.

Others say that OPEC+'s decision is a sign of complacency and risk, as it ignores the potential downside scenarios that could derail the demand recovery and push prices lower. They warn that this could backfire on OPEC+, as it could lose market share to other producers who are ramping up their output, such as the US shale industry. They also caution that this could hurt consumers, especially in developing countries that rely on imported oil and are facing inflationary pressures.

So, who is right? Well, only time will tell. As always, the oil market is unpredictable and subject to many factors that are beyond OPEC+'s control. The group may have to adjust its plans again in the future if the situation changes significantly. For now, we can only watch and wait.

That's all for today's Oil Watch. Thank you for reading and stay tuned for more updates on the world of oil and energy. Until next time!
